Background

What is Mathlib Atlas?

Mathlib is the world's largest library of formally verified mathematics, written in Lean 4. Every theorem in Mathlib has been checked by a proof assistant down to foundational axioms — making it a uniquely trustworthy dataset for studying how mathematics is actually structured. Proof formalization libraries like mathlib provide an exciting array of new possibilities, including having a centralized locus of verified mathematical knowledge, training data for LLMs and proof formalizers. These developments bring to the forefront old questions in a new format regarding what mathematics is and how it is practiced and taught. Fortunately, I think that libraries of formalized theorems can help us gain empirical evidence towards answering these questions more satisfactorily. This project is a very small step into what I believe will be a field of increasing importance, namely mathematical metaanalysis.

🔗

Proof Dependency Graph

Each theorem that uses another creates a directed edge. We construct the complete DAG of 387K edges, enabling graph-theoretic analysis of mathematical structure.

📊

Statistical Analysis

We compute in-degree, out-degree, hub scores, longest dependency chains, and BFS depths — then correlate these with proof complexity metrics.

🏷️

Named Theorem Detection

We identify theorems named after mathematicians using two independent signals: docstring bold-markup and camelCase identifier decomposition against a curated name list.

We construct the full transitive dependency graph across all of Mathlib, join it with docstrings and source positions from GitHub, and run statistical analyses comparing named vs. unnamed theorems.

Graph Metrics

For each theorem we compute: in-degree (how many proofs cite it), out-degree (how many lemmas it uses), hub score (in × out), BFS depth, and longest dependency chain via Kahn's topological DP.

Named Theorem Detection

Two independent signals: (1) doc bold **...** markup in docstrings, (2) id camelCase/underscore decomposition against ~90 mathematician names. Combined: 6,056 named theorems identified.

Docstring Harvesting

We fetch raw Lean source files from GitHub at a fixed commit hash, parse /-- ... -/ docstring blocks using source line positions from the lean_source table. 43,480 docstrings collected across all of Mathlib.

Statistical Testing

Mann-Whitney U tests (non-parametric) with rank-biserial correlation as effect size. Compares named vs. unnamed theorems across in-degree, out-degree, tactic step count, and code line count.

Results · Theorem Importance

Most Cited Theorems in Mathlib

In-degree (the number of other proofs that directly depend on a theorem) is the most direct proxy for mathematical importance within a formal library. In the future, this analysis can be expanded to perform more sophisticated statistical analyses and make large AI generated theorem libraries human interpretable.

mul_comm (commutativity of multiplication) is cited by 1,462 other proofs — more than any other result in all of Mathlib. The top 5 are all basic ring axioms, appearing more often than any named theorem like Cauchy's or Fermat's.

Results · Named Theorems

Which Named Theorems Matter Most?

We identify theorems named after mathematicians using two independent signals, then measure whether they are structurally more important than unnamed results. The answer is yes — strongly.

de Morgan's laws (not_and_or) top the named-theorem rankings with an in-degree of 113 — cited by 113 other proofs. They're followed by the Squeeze theorem (18), Monotone Convergence (16), and the Binomial theorem (16).

Results · Graph Structure

The Shape of Mathematical Knowledge

Despite hundreds of transitive dependencies, proof chains in Mathlib are surprisingly shallow. Even the most complex theorems have longest chains of only 10–15 steps (again this is partly due to the limitation in detecting dependency between very simple lemmas).

🌊

Shallow Chains

PadicInt.norm_int_lt_one_iff_dvd has 195 transitive dependencies yet its longest chain is only 12 steps. Formal proofs are wide, not deep.

🌳

87% Primitive Leaves

644,656 declarations have no outgoing edges — proved by automation (omega, simp, decide) with no recorded sub-lemmas. They form the vast forest floor of formal math.

🧲

Algebra is the Attractor

Analysis, RingTheory, LinearAlgebra, and NumberTheory all flow into Algebra at 3,000–12,000 edges each. It's the gravitational center of Mathlib's dependency structure.

Broader Context

Mathematics in the Age of Formal Verification

Why does mapping the structure of formal proofs matter beyond the library itself? The essay below situates this project in the emerging landscape of AI-assisted theorem proving, automated conjecture generation, and the future of mathematical knowledge.

📝

Essay: Framing the New Mathematical Paradigm

This project grew from a conviction that formal proof libraries like Mathlib are not just verification tools — they are the first machine-readable maps of mathematical knowledge, with graph structure that encodes centuries of mathematical intuition. Understanding which theorems are truly foundational, how mathematical areas interlock, and what distinguishes named theorems from the vast anonymous sea of lemmas, has implications for AI systems that reason about mathematics, for curriculum design, and for how we understand the sociology of mathematical discovery.
